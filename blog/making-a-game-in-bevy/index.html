Making a Game in Bevy

DATE

STILL WIP, I created an online multiplayer action game in 6 weeks. During this time, I learned a tone about how to make a game and get it done. In this blog post I summarize my experience of the development of Ace of the Heavens.

Introduction

Before jumping into the meat of this, I would like to give some perspective. I developed this game in 6 weeks, in roughly 200 hours. The engine I used is the bevy engine, written in the Rust programming language.

This was certainly not my first attempt at making a video game, I started making games more then 5 years ago. So it took me 5 years to really publish my first ever game. There were many failed attempts and I think there are a lot of reasons why I was able to push through with this game and not with the ones before. In the following sections, I will do my best at explaining what I did and provide you with actionable steps you can take to improve you game development journy.

Define a Deadline

I believe that one of the reasons I managed to pull through with this project was because I had a very clear deadline. I had exactly 150 days to achieve something of value in game development for a few reasons that I won't get into here as they don't matter, the point is that I had to finish the project.

Having a clear deadline and being aware that there is no time to lose kept my momentum up and I was focused on developing the features that would be actually useful for the game. It also kept me away from wasting my time on yak shaving. This was key in developing an MPV as fast as possible. I also forced me to have a very clear vision of the game in mind at all times, which made it possible for me to cut many corners and create the architecture of the project for the exact scope I wanted the game to be.

Have a Clear Vision

As mentioned earlier, I knew very well what kind of game I was going to create. This was crucial, as it allowed me to write code much simpler code which most would consider a bad practice because it is rigid and non extendible. However it there are two reasons this is a good idea for a beginner:

  1. You won't need the extensible code anyways if you don't even get out of prototype stage.
  2. If you know your game's scope, you will be able to cut corners and write simpler systems.

Keeping the scope small is probably the most common advise for new game devs, but it's for a good reason. If you plan to create a huge game that you know would take years to complete then your code base must be very maintainable. If it isn't, you are likely going to lose interest at some point because it's just too hard to add new features, features you didn't account for when writing the code.

Always Know Your Next Step

Everyday, once I finish my work day, I open a file where I store all my current tasks and update it (add new ones and delete completed ones). Afterwards I think for a moment about my next tasks for a given project. The next time I am ready to start work I just take a look at this file and know exactly what I need to work on.

Note that I am not telling you to plan every single little tasks for the next 3 years. Your next one or two steps are enough (so the next 30min - 3h should be planned). I don't believe there is a lot of value in over planning or other forms of meta knowledge. In my personal experience it usually only resulted in a waste of time. One or two steps is all you need (at first).

Restructure Restructure Restructure

I cannot tell you how important it is to restructure your game over and over if you want to to live for more then just a few weeks. Even if you know exactly how your game will look in the end, it's still important to regularly restructure your code-base as you learn more and more techniques and general best practices. Also keeping files small is very important for me, my motivation to work in a file that is larger then 300 lines drops exponentially fast. As game development is an inherently stateful process, keeping your files small and concise is very important.

Use Assets From Others Whenever Possible

If you are primairly a programmer like me, I highly advise you to not create every single art asset on your own. There are so many good CC0 licensed assets that you really don't need to make them yourself. Of course, if you are an artist then this doesn't really apply to you, but even in this case you might want to consider using existing art to speed up the process of the development.

  • Regularly playtest your game to gather feedback.
  • Avoid attempting peer-to-peer (P2P) development as a solo endeavor.
  • Prioritize adding engaging elements to your game early (juicing it up).
  • Don't get bogged down with fixing non-critical bugs or over-architecting (apply the 20/80 rule).
  • Avoid excessive frustration and burnout; take regular breaks, especially during challenging times.
  • Have the right mindset when developing games
